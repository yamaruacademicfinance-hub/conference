We investigate the spillover effects of the opioid epidemic on consumer finance: delinquency, bank consumer portfolio risk, and credit supply. Using multiple datasets and instruments capturing the pharmaceutical industry’s opioid marketing intensity, we uncover unfavorable credit consequences for consumers living in high-exposed areas and banks operating there. Specifically, low-creditscore consumers in areas with high opioid exposure are more likely to default on their credit obligations. Banks with higher opioid-crisis-exposure incur larger consumer non-performing loans and charge-offs. In response, banks contract credit supply to consumers in these areas, applying stricter credit terms and reducing credit offers. This contraction disproportionately affects riskier, minority, and younger consumers.

After decades of low and stable inflation in the United States (and beyond), 2021 inaugurated a surge of rapid price growth not experienced since the early 1980s. Initially viewed by many as a transitory phenomenon that would recede on its own, inflation has surprised by proving remarkably durable, prompting both a shift in public sentiment and the policy stance of the Federal Reserve. Starting in late 2021, the Federal Reserve began engaging in forward guidance by aggressively signaling future monetary tightening, which immediately drove up mortgage rates and curtailed credit supply for housing. This paper undertakes several tasks. First, it evaluates the aggregate and redistributive consequences of the Great Inflation of 2021 and 2022 via labor, housing, and mortgage markets. The sluggishness of wage adjustment led to a decline in real purchasing power, which hurt renters and owners alike. At the same time, existing homeowners have benefited from the erosion of the real value of their outstanding mortgage debt via high inflation. In contrast, prospective homeowners have had to contend with rising mortgage rates, forcing many to purchase a smaller home than previously desired or pricing them out of the owner-occupied market entirely, thus driving up rent pressures. The dramatic rise in mortgage rates also constrains the existing housing supply as current owners are reluctant to sell, given that doing so means they will have to give up their current ultra-low mortgage rate that they locked in before the rapid rise in inflation.

The Cross-Section of Housing Returns
Authors: Jonathan Halket (Texas A&M University), Lara Loewenstein (Federal Reserve Bank of Cleveland), Paul Willen (Federal Reserve Bank of Boston)
📄 PDF

我々は、米国の都市圏における一戸建て住宅の利回りに大きな系統的なばらつきがあることを実証している。低所得、低クレジットスコア、または黒人居住者の割合が高い地域は利回りが高く、したがってリターンも高い。信用力の低い地域と高い地域との間の利回りのスプレッドは、信用力の低い時期に大幅に拡大した。このため、サンプルでは、リターンの高い地域はリスクも高くなる。しかし、我々は、一部の地域が獲得している過剰リターンは、純粋に余分なリスクを負担することに対する補償ではなく、むしろ、地域の割引率が地域の資産を価格決定する、細分化された住宅市場の証拠であると主張する。

We document large systematic variations in the return to single-family residential property within U.S. metropolitan areas. Areas with low income, low credit scores or high shares of black residents have higher yields and therefore higher returns. Yield spreads between low credit areas and high credit areas widened considerably during periods when credit availability was low. This causes the areas with higher returns to also have higher risk, in sample. However we argue that the excess return that some areas earn is not purely compensation for bearing extra risk but is rather evidence for segmented housing markets where different local discount rates price local assets.

Housing Wealth and Overpayment: When Money Moves In
Authors: Darren Aiello (Brigham Young University), Jason Kotter (Brigham Young University), Gregor Schubert (University of California-Los Angeles)
📄 PDF

その結果、住宅不動産市場における情報摩擦により、外生的な住宅富を持つ転居者は、時間的に変動する地域価格や不動産自体の時間不変的な特性に対して、次の住宅を購入する際に過大な支払いをしていることがわかった。このような住宅資産に起因する過大支払いは、周辺地域により大きなプラスの価格影響と関連しており、非地元的な引越し者と比較して、地域的な引越し者の方がより大きい。住宅富の流入の総体的な効果は、通勤圏レベルの住宅価格の伸びを増加させることである。

We construct a novel dataset tracking households across property purchases covering 25 years of moves within the U.S. We find that information frictions in residential real estate markets cause movers with larger exogenous housing wealth to overpay for their next house, relative to both time varying local prices as well as time invariant characteristics of the property itself. These housing wealth driven overpayments are associated with larger positive price impacts to the surrounding neighborhood and are larger for local movers relative to non-local movers. The aggregate effect of housing wealth inflows is to increase commuting-zone-level house price growth.
